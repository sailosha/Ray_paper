Spectral Radiation Map

← back

Each instrument is plotted like an isotope on a radiation survey. Every harmonic in its spectrum becomes a glowing point source whose color is its frequency (low Hz = red, mid = yellow / green, high = blue / violet, mapped log-scaled across 150–2000 Hz), whose position on the ring is also its frequency (same log scale, going clockwise from 12 o'clock), and whose glow size and brightness follow its acoustic amplitude. Reading the three panels side by side, the differences in spectral fingerprint between the instruments become immediately visible.

How to read it. Each colored hotspot is one overtone. Because the position and color both encode frequency, the same frequency always lands at the same place with the same color across all three maps — so comparing two instruments is a matter of asking which positions light up, and how brightly. A bell with one dominant fundamental will show one huge blob and a few faint outriders; a cymbal or gong with several near-equal harmonics will show a constellation of comparably-sized blobs. Intensities are normalized within each panel so even quiet harmonics stay visible — the bottom-of-panel readout (peak XX.X dB) preserves the cross-instrument loudness comparison.
